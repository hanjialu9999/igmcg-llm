What is a noun?
A noun is a word that names a person, place, thing, or idea.
What is a verb?
A verb is a word that expresses an action or state of being.
What is an adjective?
An adjective is a word that describes or modifies a noun.
What is an adverb?
An adverb is a word that modifies a verb, adjective, or another adverb.
What is a pronoun?
A pronoun is a word that replaces a noun to avoid repetition.
What are the subject pronouns in English?
The subject pronouns are I, you, he, she, it, we, and they.
What are the object pronouns in English?
The object pronouns are me, you, him, her, it, us, and them.
What is a preposition?
A preposition is a word that shows the relationship between a noun and other words in a sentence.
What are common prepositions?
Common prepositions include in, on, at, by, with, from, to, about, and for.
What is a conjunction?
A conjunction is a word that connects words, phrases, or clauses.
What are coordinating conjunctions?
Coordinating conjunctions are for, and, nor, but, or, yet, and so, remembered by the acronym FANBOYS.
What is an article?
An article is a word used before a noun to indicate whether it is specific or general.
What are the articles in English?
The articles in English are a, an, and the.
When do we use the article a?
We use a before words that begin with a consonant sound.
When do we use the article an?
We use an before words that begin with a vowel sound.
When do we use the article the?
We use the to refer to specific or previously mentioned nouns.
What is the simple present tense?
The simple present tense describes habitual actions, general truths, or current states.
How do we form the simple present tense?
For most verbs, use the base form, adding s or es for third person singular subjects.
What is an example of simple present tense?
She walks to school every day.
What is the present continuous tense?
The present continuous tense describes actions happening now or temporary situations.
How do we form the present continuous tense?
Use the verb to be in present tense plus the verb with ing ending.
What is an example of present continuous tense?
I am studying English right now.
What is the simple past tense?
The simple past tense describes completed actions in the past.
How do we form the simple past tense for regular verbs?
Add ed to the base form of regular verbs.
What is an example of simple past tense?
He visited Paris last summer.
What are irregular verbs?
Irregular verbs do not follow the standard ed pattern for past tense.
Give three examples of irregular verbs.
Go becomes went, eat becomes ate, and see becomes saw.
What is the past continuous tense?
The past continuous tense describes actions in progress at a specific time in the past.
How do we form the past continuous tense?
Use was or were plus the verb with ing ending.
What is an example of past continuous tense?
They were watching television when I called.
What is the present perfect tense?
The present perfect tense connects past actions to the present or describes experiences up to now.
How do we form the present perfect tense?
Use have or has plus the past participle of the verb.
What is an example of present perfect tense?
I have lived here for five years.
What is the difference between present perfect and simple past?
Present perfect connects to the present while simple past refers to a specific completed time.
What is the past perfect tense?
The past perfect tense describes an action completed before another past action.
How do we form the past perfect tense?
Use had plus the past participle of the verb.
What is an example of past perfect tense?
She had finished her homework before dinner started.
What is the future simple tense?
The future simple tense describes actions that will happen in the future.
How do we form the future simple tense?
Use will or shall plus the base form of the verb.
What is an example of future simple tense?
We will travel to Japan next year.
What is another way to express future?
We can use going to plus the base verb to express future plans or intentions.
What is an example using going to?
I am going to study abroad next semester.
What is the future continuous tense?
The future continuous tense describes actions that will be in progress at a specific future time.
How do we form the future continuous tense?
Use will be plus the verb with ing ending.
What is an example of future continuous tense?
This time tomorrow, I will be flying to London.
What is the future perfect tense?
The future perfect tense describes actions that will be completed before a specific future time.
How do we form the future perfect tense?
Use will have plus the past participle of the verb.
What is an example of future perfect tense?
By next month, she will have graduated from university.
What is a modal verb?
A modal verb expresses ability, possibility, permission, or obligation.
What are common modal verbs?
Common modal verbs include can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, and would.
What does can express?
Can expresses ability or possibility.
What does must express?
Must expresses strong obligation or logical conclusion.
What does should express?
Should expresses advice or recommendation.
What is the difference between can and could?
Can refers to present ability while could refers to past ability or polite requests.
What is a sentence?
A sentence is a group of words that expresses a complete thought.
What is a simple sentence?
A simple sentence contains one independent clause with a subject and verb.
What is a compound sentence?
A compound sentence contains two or more independent clauses joined by a conjunction.
What is a complex sentence?
A complex sentence contains one independent clause and at least one dependent clause.
What is a compound-complex sentence?
A compound-complex sentence contains at least two independent clauses and one dependent clause.
What is an independent clause?
An independent clause expresses a complete thought and can stand alone as a sentence.
What is a dependent clause?
A dependent clause cannot stand alone and depends on an independent clause for meaning.
What is a subordinating conjunction?
A subordinating conjunction introduces a dependent clause, such as because, although, if, when, and while.
What is subject-verb agreement?
Subject-verb agreement means the verb must match the subject in number and person.
Give an example of subject-verb agreement.
The dog runs in the park, but the dogs run in the park.
What is a countable noun?
A countable noun can be counted and has both singular and plural forms.
What is an uncountable noun?
An uncountable noun cannot be counted and typically has no plural form.
Give examples of uncountable nouns.
Water, rice, information, advice, and furniture are uncountable nouns.
How do we express quantity with uncountable nouns?
We use phrases like a glass of water, a piece of advice, or some information.
What is the difference between much and many?
Much is used with uncountable nouns while many is used with countable nouns.
What is the difference between few and little?
Few is used with countable nouns while little is used with uncountable nouns.
What does a few mean?
A few means some, indicating a small positive quantity of countable nouns.
What does few mean?
Few means not many, indicating a small negative quantity of countable nouns.
What is the comparative form?
The comparative form compares two things using er or more.
How do we form comparatives with short adjectives?
Add er to the adjective, such as taller, faster, or smarter.
How do we form comparatives with long adjectives?
Use more before the adjective, such as more beautiful or more interesting.
What is the superlative form?
The superlative form describes the extreme quality among three or more things.
How do we form superlatives with short adjectives?
Add est to the adjective and use the, such as the tallest or the fastest.
How do we form superlatives with long adjectives?
Use the most before the adjective, such as the most beautiful or the most interesting.
What are irregular comparative and superlative forms?
Good becomes better and best, bad becomes worse and worst, and far becomes farther and farthest.
What is the difference between than and then?
Than is used in comparisons while then refers to time or sequence.
What is an infinitive?
An infinitive is the base form of a verb usually preceded by to.
What is a gerund?
A gerund is a verb form ending in ing that functions as a noun.
What is the difference between infinitive and gerund?
Some verbs take infinitives, some take gerunds, and some take both with different meanings.
Give an example of a verb followed by infinitive.
I want to learn Spanish.
Give an example of a verb followed by gerund.
She enjoys reading books.
What verb changes meaning with infinitive versus gerund?
Stop followed by infinitive means stop one activity to do another, while stop followed by gerund means cease doing something.
What is the passive voice?
The passive voice emphasizes the action or receiver rather than the doer.
How do we form the passive voice?
Use the appropriate form of be plus the past participle of the main verb.
What is an example of passive voice?
The book was written by the author.
When should we use passive voice?
Use passive voice when the doer is unknown, unimportant, or obvious from context.
What is active voice?
Active voice emphasizes the subject performing the action.
What is an example of active voice?
The author wrote the book.
What is direct speech?
Direct speech quotes the exact words someone said using quotation marks.
What is an example of direct speech?
She said, I am tired.
What is indirect speech?
Indirect speech reports what someone said without using exact words or quotation marks.
What is an example of indirect speech?
She said that she was tired.
What changes when converting direct to indirect speech?
Pronouns, tense, and time expressions often change to match the reporting context.
What is a relative clause?
A relative clause provides additional information about a noun using relative pronouns.
What are relative pronouns?
Relative pronouns include who, whom, whose, which, and that.
When do we use who?
Use who for people as the subject of the relative clause.
When do we use which?
Use which for things or animals.
When do we use that?
Use that for people or things in defining relative clauses.
What is a defining relative clause?
A defining relative clause provides essential information to identify the noun and has no commas.
What is a non-defining relative clause?
A non-defining relative clause adds extra information and is set off by commas.
What is a conditional sentence?
A conditional sentence expresses a condition and its result using if clauses.
What is the zero conditional?
The zero conditional expresses general truths using present tense in both clauses.
What is an example of zero conditional?
If you heat water to one hundred degrees Celsius, it boils.
What is the first conditional?
The first conditional expresses real or likely future situations using if plus present tense and will.
What is an example of first conditional?
If it rains tomorrow, we will stay home.
What is the second conditional?
The second conditional expresses hypothetical or unlikely present or future situations.
How do we form the second conditional?
Use if plus past simple and would plus base verb.
What is an example of second conditional?
If I won the lottery, I would travel around the world.
What is the third conditional?
The third conditional expresses hypothetical past situations and their imagined results.
How do we form the third conditional?
Use if plus past perfect and would have plus past participle.
What is an example of third conditional?
If I had studied harder, I would have passed the exam.
What is a phrasal verb?
A phrasal verb combines a verb with a preposition or adverb to create a new meaning.
Give three examples of phrasal verbs.
Look after means take care of, give up means quit, and run into means meet unexpectedly.
What is a separable phrasal verb?
A separable phrasal verb allows an object to come between the verb and particle.
What is an example of separable phrasal verb?
Turn off the light or turn the light off.
What is an inseparable phrasal verb?
An inseparable phrasal verb keeps the verb and particle together.
What is an example of inseparable phrasal verb?
Look after my cat, not look my cat after.
What is the difference between make and do?
Make typically creates or produces something while do refers to actions or activities.
Give examples using make.
Make a cake, make a decision, make progress, and make a mistake.
Give examples using do.
Do homework, do exercise, do business, and do the dishes.
What is the difference between say and tell?
Say focuses on words spoken while tell requires an object indicating who receives the information.
What is the difference between hear and listen?
Hear is involuntary perception of sound while listen is intentional attention to sound.
What is the difference between see, look, and watch?
See is involuntary visual perception, look is directing eyes toward something, and watch is observing something over time.
What is the difference between bring and take?
Bring indicates movement toward the speaker while take indicates movement away from the speaker.
What is the difference between borrow and lend?
Borrow means receive temporarily while lend means give temporarily.
What is the difference between its and it's?
Its is the possessive form while it's is the contraction of it is or it has.
What is the difference between your and you're?
Your is the possessive form while you're is the contraction of you are.
What is the difference between their, there, and they're?
Their is possessive, there indicates location, and they're is the contraction of they are.
What is the difference between to, too, and two?
To is a preposition, too means also or excessively, and two is the number.
What is the difference between affect and effect?
Affect is usually a verb meaning to influence while effect is usually a noun meaning result.
What is the difference between accept and except?
Accept means to receive or agree while except means excluding or apart from.
What is the difference between lose and loose?
Lose is a verb meaning to misplace or fail to win while loose is an adjective meaning not tight.
What is subject complement?
A subject complement follows a linking verb and describes or renames the subject.
What is object complement?
An object complement follows and modifies or renames the direct object.
What are linking verbs?
Linking verbs connect the subject to information about it, such as be, seem, become, appear, and feel.
What is a transitive verb?
A transitive verb requires a direct object to complete its meaning.
What is an intransitive verb?
An intransitive verb does not take a direct object.
What is a direct object?
A direct object receives the action of the verb and answers what or whom.
What is an indirect object?
An indirect object receives the direct object and answers to whom or for whom.
What is parallel structure?
Parallel structure uses the same grammatical form for items in a list or comparison.
Give an example of parallel structure.

What is a modifier?
A modifier is a word or phrase that describes or limits another word.
What is a misplaced modifier?
A misplaced modifier is positioned too far from the word it modifies causing confusion.
What is a dangling modifier?
A dangling modifier has no clear word in the sentence to modify.
Give an example of dangling modifier error.

What is a run-on sentence?
A run-on sentence joins two independent clauses without proper punctuation or conjunction.
What is a sentence fragment?
A sentence fragment is an incomplete sentence lacking a subject, verb, or complete thought.
What is a comma splice?
A comma splice incorrectly joins two independent clauses with only a comma.
How can we fix a run-on sentence?
Use a period, semicolon, comma with conjunction, or make one clause dependent.
What is the Oxford comma?
The Oxford comma is the optional comma before the conjunction in a list of three or more items.
When do we use semicolons?
Use semicolons to join closely related independent clauses or separate complex list items.
When do we use colons?
Use colons to introduce lists, explanations, or quotes after an independent clause.
What is an apostrophe used for?
Apostrophes show possession or indicate missing letters in contractions.
How do we form possessives with singular nouns?
Add apostrophe s to singular nouns, such as the cat's toy.
How do we form possessives with plural nouns ending in s?
Add only an apostrophe after the s, such as the cats' toys.
What about plural nouns not ending in s?
Add apostrophe s, such as the children's books.
What is capitalization?
Capitalization is using uppercase letters for proper nouns and sentence beginnings.
What words should be capitalized?
Capitalize proper nouns, the first word of sentences, titles, days, months, and the pronoun I.
Should we capitalize seasons?
No, seasons like spring, summer, fall, and winter are not capitalized unless part of a title.

What is scanning?
Scanning is searching text quickly for specific information.
What is close reading?
Close reading carefully examines text details to understand deeper meanings.
What is prediction in reading?
Prediction uses clues to anticipate what will happen next in a text.
What is visualization?
Visualization creates mental images while reading to enhance comprehension.
What is making connections?
Making connections links text to personal experience, other texts, or world knowledge.
What is questioning while reading?
Questioning involves asking about characters, events, and author's purpose during reading.
What is monitoring comprehension?
Monitoring comprehension means checking understanding and fixing confusion while reading.
What is rereading?
Rereading goes through text again to clarify understanding or notice new details.
What is critical reading?
Critical reading analyzes and evaluates ideas rather than accepting them at face value.
What is literary analysis?
Literary analysis examines elements like theme, character, plot, and symbolism in literature.
What is theme?
Theme is the central message or insight about life explored in a literary work.
What is plot?
Plot is the sequence of events in a story.
What is exposition?
Exposition introduces characters, setting, and situation at the beginning of a story.
What is rising action?
Rising action builds tension through conflicts and complications moving toward the climax.
What is climax?
Climax is the turning point or moment of highest tension in a story.
What is falling action?
Falling action shows the consequences of the climax and moves toward resolution.
What is resolution?
Resolution concludes the story by resolving conflicts.
What is conflict?
Conflict is the struggle between opposing forces that drives the plot.
What is internal conflict?
Internal conflict occurs within a character's mind between desires, beliefs, or emotions.
What is external conflict?
External conflict occurs between a character and an outside force.
What is character versus character conflict?
This conflict pits one character against another.
What is character versus self conflict?
This conflict shows a character struggling with internal issues.
What is character versus society conflict?
This conflict shows a character opposing societal norms or expectations.
What is character versus nature conflict?
This conflict pits a character against natural forces or the environment.
What is character versus fate conflict?
This conflict involves a character struggling against destiny or circumstances beyond control.
What is a protagonist?
The protagonist is the main character who drives the story forward.
What is an antagonist?
The antagonist opposes the protagonist creating conflict.
What is characterization?
Characterization is the method an author uses to develop and reveal character traits.
What is direct characterization?
Direct characterization explicitly tells readers about a character's traits.
What is indirect characterization?
Indirect characterization shows traits through actions, thoughts, dialogue, and appearance.
What is a round character?
A round character is complex with multiple traits and realistic depth.
What is a flat character?
A flat character has one or two traits and little complexity.
What is a dynamic character?
A dynamic character undergoes significant internal change throughout the story.
What is a static character?
A static character remains essentially unchanged throughout the story.
What is setting?
Setting is the time and place where a story occurs.
What is mood?
Mood is the emotional atmosphere created by a literary work.
What is tone?
Tone is the author's attitude toward the subject or audience.
What is foreshadowing in literature?
Foreshadowing provides hints about future events to create anticipation.
What is flashback?
Flashback interrupts chronological sequence to show past events.
What is dramatic irony?
Dramatic irony occurs when readers know something characters do not.
What is situational irony?
Situational irony is when the outcome differs from what was expected.
What is verbal irony?
Verbal irony is saying the opposite of what is meant, often sarcastically.
What is allegory?
Allegory is a story where characters and events represent abstract ideas or moral qualities.
What is allusion?
Allusion is a brief reference to a person, event, or work from history or literature.
What is motif?
A motif is a recurring element that has symbolic significance in a story.
What is archetype?
An archetype is a universal symbol or character type found across cultures and literature.
What is prose?
Prose is ordinary written language organized in paragraphs rather than verse.
What is poetry?
Poetry uses condensed language, imagery, and often rhythm or rhyme to express ideas.
What is a stanza?
A stanza is a grouped set of lines in a poem.
What is rhyme scheme?
Rhyme scheme is the pattern of rhyming lines in a poem.
What is meter?
Meter is the rhythmic pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables in poetry.
What is iambic pentameter?
Iambic pentameter has five pairs of unstressed and stressed syllables per line.
What is free verse?
Free verse poetry has no regular meter or rhyme scheme.
What is a sonnet?
A sonnet is a fourteen-line poem with a specific rhyme scheme.
What is a haiku?
A haiku is a three-line poem with five, seven, and five syllables.
What is blank verse?
Blank verse is unrhymed poetry written in iambic pentameter.
What is enjambment?
Enjambment continues a sentence beyond the end of a line without punctuation.
What is caesura?
Caesura is a pause in the middle of a line of poetry.
What is consonance?
Consonance repeats consonant sounds within or at the end of words.
What is a refrain?
A refrain is a repeated line or phrase in a poem or song.
What is speaker in poetry?
The speaker is the voice that narrates the poem.
What is drama?
Drama is literature written to be performed by actors.
What is dialogue?
Dialogue is the conversation between characters.
What is monologue?
A monologue is a long speech by one character.
What is soliloquy?
A soliloquy is when a character speaks thoughts aloud alone on stage.
What is aside?
An aside is a brief remark by a character heard by the audience but not other characters.
What is stage directions?
Stage directions are instructions for actors and production staff about movement and setting.
What is an act?
An act is a major division in a play.
What is a scene?
A scene is a subdivision of an act showing continuous action in one place.
What is tragic hero?
A tragic hero is a noble character whose flaw leads to downfall.
What is hubris?
Hubris is excessive pride that leads to a character's downfall.
What is catharsis?
Catharsis is emotional release experienced by audience through tragedy.
What is comic relief?
Comic relief uses humor to relieve tension in a serious work.
What is genre?
Genre is a category of literature with shared characteristics.
What is fiction?
Fiction is imaginative writing not based strictly on real events.
What is nonfiction?
Nonfiction presents factual information about real people, places, and events.
What is autobiography?
Autobiography is a person's life story written by that person.
What is biography?
Biography is a life story written by someone other than the subject.
What is memoir?
A memoir focuses on specific memories or time periods from someone's life.
What is essay?
An essay is a short nonfiction work on a specific topic.
What is a personal essay?
A personal essay explores the author's experiences, feelings, and insights.
What is a formal essay?
A formal essay presents a serious argument in an impersonal, structured manner.
What is an informal essay?
An informal essay uses conversational tone and may include personal anecdotes.
What is a reflective essay?
A reflective essay examines experiences and what was learned from them.
What is a compare and contrast essay?
This essay examines similarities and differences between subjects.
What is a cause and effect essay?
This essay explains how one event leads to another.
What is a process essay?
A process essay explains how to do something or how something works.
What is a definition essay?
A definition essay explains the meaning of a term or concept in depth.
What is a classification essay?
A classification essay divides a topic into categories with examples.
What is an analytical essay?
An analytical essay breaks down a topic to examine its parts.
What is an interpretive essay?
An interpretive essay explains meaning and significance of a text.
What is a response essay?
A response essay presents personal reactions to a text or experience.
What is an evaluation essay?
An evaluation essay judges the quality or value of something based on criteria.
What is a problem-solution essay?
This essay identifies a problem and proposes solutions.
What is attribution?
Attribution identifies the source of information or quotations.
What is a signal phrase?
A signal phrase introduces a quotation or paraphrase and names the source.
What is integrating quotations?
Integrating quotations smoothly incorporates quoted material into your own sentences.
What is a block quotation?
A block quotation is a long quote set off from the text without quotation marks.
What is ellipsis in quotations?
Ellipsis uses three dots to show words omitted from a quotation.
What are brackets in quotations?
Brackets indicate changes or additions made to original quoted text.
What is a topic proposal?
A topic proposal outlines the subject and approach for a research paper.
What is a working thesis?
A working thesis is a preliminary main argument that may be revised during research.
What is a literature review?
A literature review surveys existing research on a topic.
What is an annotated bibliography?
An annotated bibliography includes citations plus brief summaries and evaluations of sources.
What is a research paper?
A research paper presents an argument supported by evidence from multiple sources.
What is academic writing?
Academic writing is formal scholarly writing for educational contexts.
What is scholarly source?
A scholarly source is peer-reviewed research written by experts.
What is a popular source?
A popular source is written for general audience by journalists or writers.
What is a database?
A database is an organized collection of information accessible electronically.
What is Boolean search?
Boolean search uses operators like AND, OR, and NOT to refine searches.
What is a keyword?
A keyword is a significant word used to search for information.
What is evaluating sources?
Evaluating sources assesses reliability, accuracy, and relevance of information.
What is the CRAAP test?
The CRAAP test evaluates sources by Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, and Purpose.
What is confirmation bias?
Confirmation bias favors information that confirms existing beliefs.
What is a logical fallacy?
A logical fallacy is an error in reasoning that weakens an argument.
What is ad hominem fallacy?
Ad hominem attacks the person rather than addressing the argument.
What is straw man fallacy?
Straw man misrepresents an opponent's argument to make it easier to attack.
What is false dilemma?
False dilemma presents only two options when more exist.
What is slippery slope?
Slippery slope argues one action will inevitably lead to extreme consequences.
What is hasty generalization?
Hasty generalization draws conclusions from insufficient evidence.
What is post hoc fallacy?
Post hoc assumes that because one event followed another, the first caused the second.
What is bandwagon fallacy?
Bandwagon argues something is correct because many people believe it.
What is appeal to authority?
Appeal to authority uses an expert opinion as proof when expertise is irrelevant.
What is red herring?
Red herring introduces irrelevant information to distract from the real issue.
What is circular reasoning?
Circular reasoning uses the conclusion as support for itself.
What is false analogy?
False analogy compares two things that are not sufficiently similar.
What is loaded language?
Loaded language uses words with strong emotional connotations to manipulate.
What is appeal to emotion?
Appeal to emotion manipulates feelings rather than using logic.
What is appeal to tradition?
Appeal to tradition argues something is good because it has always been done that way.
What is deductive reasoning?
Deductive reasoning moves from general principles to specific conclusions.
What is inductive reasoning?
Inductive reasoning moves from specific observations to general conclusions.
What is a premise?
A premise is a statement offered as evidence in an argument.
What is a conclusion in logic?
A conclusion is the claim supported by premises in an argument.
What is validity in logic?
Validity means if premises are true, the conclusion must be true.
What is soundness in logic?
Soundness means an argument is valid and has true premises.
What is a claim?
A claim is a statement that something is true and requires support.
What is evidence?
Evidence is factual information supporting a claim.
What is reasoning?
Reasoning explains how evidence supports the claim.
What is a warrant?
A warrant is the underlying assumption connecting evidence to claim.
What is a qualifier?
A qualifier limits the scope of a claim using words like usually or probably.
What is concession?
Concession acknowledges valid points in opposing arguments.
What is refutation?
Refutation proves an opposing argument wrong.
What is Toulmin model?
Toulmin model is an argumentation framework with claim, evidence, warrant, backing, qualifier, and rebuttal.
What is Rogerian argument?
Rogerian argument seeks common ground and compromise rather than winning.
What is classical argument?
Classical argument follows traditional structure with introduction, narration, confirmation, refutation, and conclusion.
What is emotional appeal?
Emotional appeal evokes feelings to persuade the audience.
What is ethical appeal?
Ethical appeal establishes credibility and trustworthiness.
What is logical appeal?
Logical appeal uses facts, statistics, and reasoning to persuade.
What is kairos?
Kairos is the rhetorical appeal to timeliness and appropriateness.
What is exigence?
Exigence is the issue or problem that prompts communication.
What is rhetorical situation?
Rhetorical situation includes writer, audience, purpose, and context.
What is rhetorical triangle?
Rhetorical triangle shows the relationship between writer, audience, and subject.
What is audience analysis?
Audience analysis considers readers' knowledge, beliefs, and needs.
What is purpose statement?
A purpose statement clearly identifies what the writing aims to accomplish.
What is a writing process?
Writing process includes prewriting, drafting, revising, editing, and publishing.
What is prewriting?
Prewriting generates and organizes ideas before drafting.
What is clustering?
Clustering creates a visual map of ideas branching from a central topic.
What is cubing?
Cubing examines a topic from six perspectives: describe, compare, associate, analyze, apply, and argue.
What is journalistic questions?
Journalistic questions are who, what, when, where, why, and how.
What is looping?
Looping is freewriting followed by selecting key ideas for further freewriting.
What is a discovery draft?
A discovery draft explores ideas without worrying about organization or correctness.
What is global revision?
Global revision addresses big-picture issues like focus, development, and organization.
What is local revision?
Local revision fixes sentence-level issues like clarity and word choice.
What is peer editing?
Peer editing involves others reviewing and suggesting improvements to writing.
What is self-editing?
Self-editing is reviewing and correcting your own work.
What is a rubric?
A rubric provides criteria and standards for evaluating writing.
What is formative assessment?
Formative assessment provides feedback during the learning process.
What is summative assessment?
Summative assessment evaluates learning at the end of a unit or course.
What is portfolio?
A portfolio is a collection of work showing development and achievement.
What is reflection in writing?
Reflection examines the writing process and what was learned.
What is metacognition?
Metacognition is thinking about one's own thinking and learning processes.
What is growth mindset?
Growth mindset believes abilities can develop through effort and learning.
What is writing fluency?
Writing fluency is the ability to produce writing smoothly and easily.
What is writer's block?
Writer's block is difficulty starting or continuing writing.
What is scaffolding in learning?
Scaffolding provides temporary support helping learners accomplish tasks.
What is zone of proximal development?
Zone of proximal development is the range between what learners can do alone and with help.
What is transfer in learning?
Transfer applies knowledge or skills learned in one context to another.
What is explicit instruction?
Explicit instruction directly teaches skills through clear explanation and modeling.
What is implicit learning?
Implicit learning acquires knowledge unconsciously through exposure and practice.
