What is biology?
Biology is the scientific study of living organisms and life processes.
What is a cell?
A cell is the basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms.
What is a prokaryotic cell?
A prokaryotic cell is a cell without a membrane-bound nucleus.
What is a eukaryotic cell?
A eukaryotic cell is a cell with a membrane-bound nucleus and organelles.
What is DNA?
DNA is deoxyribonucleic acid that carries genetic information in living organisms.
What is RNA?
RNA is ribonucleic acid that plays roles in protein synthesis and gene regulation.
What is a gene?
A gene is a segment of DNA that codes for a specific protein or RNA molecule.
What is a chromosome?
A chromosome is a structure containing tightly coiled DNA and proteins.
What is mitosis?
Mitosis is cell division producing two identical daughter cells.
What is meiosis?
Meiosis is cell division producing four genetically different gametes with half the chromosome number.
What is photosynthesis?
Photosynthesis is the process plants use to convert light energy into chemical energy.
What is cellular respiration?
Cellular respiration is the process cells use to break down glucose and produce ATP.
What is ATP?
ATP is adenosine triphosphate, the primary energy currency of cells.
What is an enzyme?
An enzyme is a biological catalyst that speeds up chemical reactions.
What is a protein?
A protein is a large molecule made of amino acids that performs various functions in organisms.
What are amino acids?
Amino acids are organic compounds that are the building blocks of proteins.
What is a lipid?
A lipid is a hydrophobic molecule including fats, oils, and steroids.
What is a carbohydrate?
A carbohydrate is an organic compound made of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen used for energy and structure.
What is a nucleic acid?
A nucleic acid is a polymer made of nucleotides including DNA and RNA.
What is the cell membrane?
The cell membrane is a phospholipid bilayer that surrounds and protects the cell.
What is the nucleus?
The nucleus is the membrane-bound organelle containing the cell's genetic material.
What are mitochondria?
Mitochondria are organelles that produce ATP through cellular respiration.
What are chloroplasts?
Chloroplasts are organelles in plant cells where photosynthesis occurs.
What is the endoplasmic reticulum?
The endoplasmic reticulum is an organelle network involved in protein and lipid synthesis.
What is the Golgi apparatus?
The Golgi apparatus modifies, packages, and transports proteins and lipids.
What are ribosomes?
Ribosomes are cellular structures where protein synthesis occurs.
What are lysosomes?
Lysosomes are organelles containing digestive enzymes that break down cellular waste.
What is the cytoplasm?
The cytoplasm is the gel-like substance filling the cell between the membrane and nucleus.
What is the cytoskeleton?
The cytoskeleton is a network of protein filaments providing cell structure and movement.
What is osmosis?
Osmosis is the diffusion of water across a semi-permeable membrane.
What is diffusion?
Diffusion is the movement of molecules from high to low concentration.
What is active transport?
Active transport is the movement of molecules against a concentration gradient using energy.
What is passive transport?
Passive transport is the movement of molecules down a concentration gradient without energy.
What is homeostasis?
Homeostasis is the maintenance of stable internal conditions in an organism.
What is metabolism?
Metabolism is the sum of all chemical reactions occurring in an organism.
What is an ecosystem?
An ecosystem is a community of organisms interacting with their physical environment.
What is a food chain?
A food chain is a linear sequence showing how energy flows from one organism to another.
What is a food web?
A food web is a complex network of interconnected food chains in an ecosystem.
What is a producer?
A producer is an organism that makes its own food through photosynthesis or chemosynthesis.
What is a consumer?
A consumer is an organism that obtains energy by eating other organisms.
What is a decomposer?
A decomposer is an organism that breaks down dead organic matter.
What is a herbivore?
A herbivore is an animal that eats only plants.
What is a carnivore?
A carnivore is an animal that eats only other animals.
What is an omnivore?
An omnivore is an animal that eats both plants and animals.
What is a species?
A species is a group of organisms that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring.
What is taxonomy?
Taxonomy is the science of classifying and naming organisms.
What are the three domains of life?
The three domains are Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya.
What are the five kingdoms of life?
The five kingdoms are Monera, Protista, Fungi, Plantae, and Animalia.
What is evolution?
Evolution is the change in heritable characteristics of populations over successive generations.
What is natural selection?
Natural selection is the process where organisms better adapted to their environment survive and reproduce more.
Who proposed the theory of evolution?
Charles Darwin proposed the theory of evolution by natural selection.
What is adaptation?
Adaptation is a trait that increases an organism's fitness in its environment.
What is fitness in biology?
Fitness is an organism's ability to survive and reproduce in its environment.
What is a mutation?
A mutation is a change in the DNA sequence of a gene.
What is genetic variation?
Genetic variation is the diversity in gene frequencies within a population.
What is heredity?
Heredity is the passing of traits from parents to offspring through genes.
What is Mendel's law of segregation?
The law of segregation states that allele pairs separate during gamete formation.
What is Mendel's law of independent assortment?
Independent assortment states that genes for different traits are inherited independently.
What is a genotype?
A genotype is an organism's genetic makeup.
What is a phenotype?
A phenotype is an organism's observable characteristics.
What is a dominant allele?
A dominant allele is expressed when at least one copy is present.
What is a recessive allele?
A recessive allele is only expressed when two copies are present.
What is incomplete dominance?
Incomplete dominance occurs when neither allele is completely dominant producing a blended phenotype.
What is codominance?
Codominance occurs when both alleles are fully expressed in the phenotype.
What is a heterozygote?
A heterozygote has two different alleles for a gene.
What is a homozygote?
A homozygote has two identical alleles for a gene.
What is a Punnett square?
A Punnett square is a diagram used to predict offspring genotypes.
What is linkage in genetics?
Linkage is the tendency of genes located close together on a chromosome to be inherited together.
What is crossing over?
Crossing over is the exchange of genetic material between homologous chromosomes during meiosis.
What is genetic recombination?
Genetic recombination is the production of offspring with new combinations of genes.
What is sex-linked inheritance?
Sex-linked inheritance involves genes located on sex chromosomes.
What are autosomes?
Autosomes are chromosomes that are not sex chromosomes.
What is a karyotype?
A karyotype is a display of all chromosomes in a cell arranged by size and shape.
What is polyploidy?
Polyploidy is having more than two complete sets of chromosomes.
What is aneuploidy?
Aneuploidy is having an abnormal number of chromosomes.
What is Down syndrome?
Down syndrome is a genetic disorder caused by an extra copy of chromosome twenty-one.
What is a pedigree?
A pedigree is a diagram showing the inheritance of a trait through generations of a family.
What is transcription?
Transcription is the synthesis of RNA from a DNA template.
What is translation?
Translation is the synthesis of proteins from an mRNA template.
What is the central dogma of molecular biology?
The central dogma describes information flow from DNA to RNA to protein.
What is a codon?
A codon is a three-nucleotide sequence that codes for a specific amino acid.
What is the genetic code?
The genetic code is the set of rules by which DNA sequences are translated into proteins.
What is a start codon?
A start codon is AUG that signals where translation should begin.
What are stop codons?
Stop codons are UAA, UAG, and UGA that signal where translation should end.
What is messenger RNA?
Messenger RNA carries genetic information from DNA to ribosomes.
What is transfer RNA?
Transfer RNA brings amino acids to ribosomes during protein synthesis.
What is ribosomal RNA?
Ribosomal RNA is a structural and catalytic component of ribosomes.
What is a promoter?
A promoter is a DNA region where RNA polymerase binds to initiate transcription.
What is RNA polymerase?
RNA polymerase is the enzyme that synthesizes RNA from a DNA template.
What is splicing?
Splicing is the removal of introns and joining of exons in pre-mRNA.
What are introns?
Introns are non-coding sequences removed from pre-mRNA during splicing.
What are exons?
Exons are coding sequences that remain in mature mRNA after splicing.
What is alternative splicing?
Alternative splicing produces different proteins from the same gene by including different exons.
What is gene expression?
Gene expression is the process by which genetic information is used to synthesize functional products.
What is gene regulation?
Gene regulation controls when and how much a gene is expressed.
What are transcription factors?
Transcription factors are proteins that regulate gene transcription by binding to DNA.
What is an operon?
An operon is a cluster of genes under control of a single promoter in prokaryotes.
What is the lac operon?
The lac operon regulates genes for lactose metabolism in bacteria.
What is epigenetics?
Epigenetics studies heritable changes in gene expression without changes to DNA sequence.
What is DNA methylation?
DNA methylation is the addition of methyl groups to DNA that can silence gene expression.
What is histone modification?
Histone modification alters chromatin structure affecting gene accessibility.
What is chromatin?
Chromatin is the complex of DNA and proteins that makes up chromosomes.
What is heterochromatin?
Heterochromatin is tightly packed chromatin that is transcriptionally inactive.
What is euchromatin?
Euchromatin is loosely packed chromatin that is transcriptionally active.
What is DNA replication?
DNA replication is the process of copying DNA before cell division.
What is semiconservative replication?
Semiconservative replication produces two DNA molecules each with one old and one new strand.
What is DNA polymerase?
DNA polymerase is the enzyme that synthesizes new DNA strands.
What is a replication fork?
A replication fork is the Y-shaped structure where DNA strands separate during replication.
What is a leading strand?
The leading strand is synthesized continuously in the direction of replication fork movement.
What is a lagging strand?
The lagging strand is synthesized discontinuously in short fragments away from fork movement.
What are Okazaki fragments?
Okazaki fragments are short DNA segments synthesized on the lagging strand.
What is DNA ligase?
DNA ligase is the enzyme that joins Okazaki fragments together.
What is helicase?
Helicase is the enzyme that unwinds the DNA double helix during replication.
What is primase?
Primase is the enzyme that synthesizes RNA primers needed to start DNA synthesis.
What is a telomere?
A telomere is a protective cap of repetitive DNA at the end of chromosomes.
What is telomerase?
Telomerase is an enzyme that adds telomeric sequences to chromosome ends.
What is PCR?
PCR is polymerase chain reaction, a technique to amplify specific DNA sequences.
What is gel electrophoresis?
Gel electrophoresis separates DNA fragments by size using an electric field.
What is DNA sequencing?
DNA sequencing determines the precise order of nucleotides in a DNA molecule.
What is recombinant DNA?
Recombinant DNA is DNA formed by combining sequences from different sources.
What is a plasmid?
A plasmid is a small circular DNA molecule in bacteria used in genetic engineering.
What is a restriction enzyme?
A restriction enzyme cuts DNA at specific recognition sequences.
What is gene cloning?
Gene cloning is the process of making multiple copies of a gene.
What is transformation in bacteria?
Transformation is the uptake of foreign DNA by bacterial cells.
What is CRISPR?
CRISPR is a gene-editing technology that allows precise modification of DNA sequences.
What is genome?
A genome is the complete set of genetic material in an organism.
What is the Human Genome Project?
The Human Genome Project mapped all human genes and their DNA sequences.
What is genomics?
Genomics is the study of entire genomes including gene interactions.
What is proteomics?
Proteomics is the study of all proteins expressed by a genome.
What is bioinformatics?
Bioinformatics uses computational tools to analyze biological data.
What is a transgenic organism?
A transgenic organism contains DNA from another species.
What is gene therapy?
Gene therapy treats disease by introducing corrected genes into patient cells.
What is a stem cell?
A stem cell can divide and differentiate into various specialized cell types.
What is cellular differentiation?
Cellular differentiation is the process by which cells become specialized.
What is apoptosis?
Apoptosis is programmed cell death.
What is the cell cycle?
The cell cycle is the series of events cells go through from formation to division.
What are the phases of the cell cycle?
The phases are G1, S, G2, and M phase.
What is interphase?
Interphase includes G1, S, and G2 phases when the cell grows and replicates DNA.
What is the G1 phase?
G1 phase is when the cell grows and prepares for DNA synthesis.
What is the S phase?
S phase is when DNA replication occurs.
What is the G2 phase?
G2 phase is when the cell prepares for mitosis.
What is the M phase?
M phase includes mitosis and cytokinesis when the cell divides.
What are cyclins?
Cyclins are proteins that regulate progression through the cell cycle.
What are checkpoints in the cell cycle?
Checkpoints are control points that ensure proper cell cycle progression.
What is cancer?
Cancer is uncontrolled cell division resulting from mutations in genes regulating the cell cycle.
What is a tumor suppressor gene?
A tumor suppressor gene is a gene that normally prevents uncontrolled cell growth.
What is an oncogene?
An oncogene is a mutated gene that promotes cancer development.
What is metastasis?
Metastasis is the spread of cancer cells from the original tumor to other body parts.
What is a carcinogen?
A carcinogen is a substance that can cause cancer.
What is tissue?
Tissue is a group of similar cells working together to perform a function.
What are the four main tissue types?
The four main types are epithelial, connective, muscle, and nervous tissue.
What is epithelial tissue?
Epithelial tissue covers body surfaces and lines organs and cavities.
What is connective tissue?
Connective tissue supports, binds, and protects other tissues and organs.
What is muscle tissue?
Muscle tissue is specialized for contraction and movement.
What is nervous tissue?
Nervous tissue transmits electrical signals throughout the body.
What is an organ?
An organ is a structure composed of multiple tissue types performing specific functions.
What is an organ system?
An organ system is a group of organs working together to perform major body functions.
What is the circulatory system?
The circulatory system transports blood, nutrients, oxygen, and waste throughout the body.
What is the respiratory system?
The respiratory system enables gas exchange between the body and environment.
What is the digestive system?
The digestive system breaks down food and absorbs nutrients.
What is the nervous system?
The nervous system coordinates body activities through electrical signals.
What is the endocrine system?
The endocrine system regulates body functions through hormones.
What is the immune system?
The immune system defends the body against pathogens and disease.
What is the skeletal system?
The skeletal system provides structure, support, and protection for the body.
What is the muscular system?
The muscular system enables body movement and maintains posture.
What is the urinary system?
The urinary system removes waste and regulates water and electrolyte balance.
What is the reproductive system?
The reproductive system produces gametes and enables reproduction.
What is a hormone?
A hormone is a chemical messenger secreted by endocrine glands that regulates body functions.
What is insulin?
Insulin is a hormone that regulates blood glucose levels.
What is testosterone?
Testosterone is the primary male sex hormone.
What is estrogen?
Estrogen is a primary female sex hormone.
What is a neuron?
A neuron is a specialized cell that transmits electrical signals in the nervous system.
What is a synapse?
A synapse is the junction between two neurons where signals are transmitted.
What is a neurotransmitter?
A neurotransmitter is a chemical messenger that transmits signals across synapses.
What is the central nervous system?
The central nervous system consists of the brain and spinal cord.
What is the peripheral nervous system?
The peripheral nervous system consists of nerves outside the brain and spinal cord.
What is the autonomic nervous system?
The autonomic nervous system controls involuntary body functions.
What is the somatic nervous system?
The somatic nervous system controls voluntary movements.
What is the sympathetic nervous system?
The sympathetic nervous system prepares the body for stress or emergency.
What is the parasympathetic nervous system?
The parasympathetic nervous system promotes rest and digestion.
What is an action potential?
An action potential is a rapid electrical signal transmitted along a neuron.
What is myelin?
Myelin is an insulating layer around nerve fibers that speeds signal transmission.
What is a reflex?
A reflex is an automatic response to a stimulus.
What is innate immunity?
Innate immunity is the nonspecific defense system present from birth.
What is adaptive immunity?
Adaptive immunity is the specific immune response that develops after pathogen exposure.
What is an antibody?
An antibody is a protein produced by B cells that binds to specific antigens.
What is an antigen?
An antigen is a molecule that triggers an immune response.
What are B cells?
B cells are lymphocytes that produce antibodies in the adaptive immune response.
What are T cells?
T cells are lymphocytes that coordinate immune responses and kill infected cells.
What is a vaccine?
A vaccine stimulates immunity by exposing the body to weakened or inactive pathogens.
What is inflammation?
Inflammation is a localized immune response to injury or infection.
What is an allergy?
An allergy is an overreaction of the immune system to a harmless substance.
What is autoimmune disease?
An autoimmune disease occurs when the immune system attacks the body's own cells.
What is HIV?
HIV is human immunodeficiency virus that attacks the immune system.
What is a pathogen?
A pathogen is an organism that causes disease.
What is a virus?
A virus is a non-living infectious agent that requires a host cell to replicate.
What is a bacterium?
A bacterium is a single-celled prokaryotic microorganism.
What is a fungus?
A fungus is a eukaryotic organism that absorbs nutrients from organic matter.
What is a parasite?
A parasite is an organism that lives on or in a host and benefits at the host's expense.
What is symbiosis?
Symbiosis is a close relationship between two different species.
What is mutualism?
Mutualism is a symbiotic relationship where both species benefit.
What is commensalism?
Commensalism is a relationship where one species benefits and the other is unaffected.
What is parasitism?
Parasitism is a relationship where one species benefits at the expense of another.
What is predation?
Predation is an interaction where one organism kills and consumes another.
What is competition?
Competition occurs when organisms vie for the same limited resources.
What is a niche?
A niche is the role and position a species has in its environment.
What is a habitat?
A habitat is the physical environment where an organism lives.
What is population?
A population is a group of individuals of the same species living in the same area.
What is population density?
Population density is the number of individuals per unit area or volume.
What is carrying capacity?
Carrying capacity is the maximum population size an environment can sustain.
What is exponential growth?
Exponential growth is unlimited population increase when resources are abundant.
What is logistic growth?
Logistic growth is population increase that levels off as it approaches carrying capacity.
What is a limiting factor?
A limiting factor is a resource or condition that restricts population growth.
What is succession?
Succession is the gradual change in species composition in an ecosystem over time.
What is primary succession?
Primary succession occurs in areas with no previous soil such as bare rock.
What is secondary succession?
Secondary succession occurs in areas where soil remains after disturbance.
What is a climax community?
A climax community is a stable ecosystem that represents the endpoint of succession.
What is biodiversity?
Biodiversity is the variety of life forms in an ecosystem or on Earth.
What is species richness?
Species richness is the number of different species in an area.
What is species evenness?
Species evenness is how equally abundant different species are in an area.
What is extinction?
Extinction is the complete disappearance of a species.
What is an endangered species?
An endangered species is at high risk of extinction in the near future.
What is conservation biology?
Conservation biology studies how to protect and restore biodiversity.
What is a keystone species?
A keystone species has a disproportionately large effect on its ecosystem.
What is an invasive species?
An invasive species is a non-native organism that causes harm in a new environment.
What is biogeochemical cycle?
A biogeochemical cycle is the movement of elements through living and nonliving components of ecosystems.
What is the carbon cycle?
The carbon cycle describes the movement of carbon through Earth's systems.
What is the nitrogen cycle?
The nitrogen cycle describes the movement of nitrogen through Earth's systems.
What is nitrogen fixation?
Nitrogen fixation converts atmospheric nitrogen into forms usable by organisms.
What is the water cycle?
The water cycle describes the continuous movement of water through evaporation, condensation, and precipitation.
What is primary productivity?
Primary productivity is the rate at which producers convert energy into organic matter.
What is gross primary productivity?
Gross primary productivity is the total amount of energy captured by producers.
What is net primary productivity?
Net primary productivity is energy available to consumers after producer respiration.
What is biomass?
Biomass is the total mass of living organisms in a given area.
What is trophic level?
A trophic level is a position in a food chain or web.
What is energy pyramid?
An energy pyramid shows energy flow through trophic levels with decreasing amounts at higher levels.
What is the ten percent rule?
The ten percent rule states that only about ten percent of energy transfers between trophic levels.
What is bioaccumulation?
Bioaccumulation is the buildup of substances in an organism over its lifetime.
What is biomagnification?
Biomagnification is the increasing concentration of substances at higher trophic levels.
What is eutrophication?
Eutrophication is excessive nutrient enrichment in water bodies causing algal blooms.
What is ecological footprint?
Ecological footprint measures human demand on natural resources.
What is speciation?
Speciation is the formation of new species from existing populations.
What is allopatric speciation?
Allopatric speciation occurs when populations are geographically separated.
What is sympatric speciation?
Sympatric speciation occurs without geographic separation within the same area.
What is reproductive isolation?
Reproductive isolation prevents interbreeding between populations.
What is prezygotic isolation?
Prezygotic isolation prevents fertilization from occurring.
What is postzygotic isolation?
Postzygotic isolation reduces hybrid viability or fertility after fertilization.
What is gene flow?
Gene flow is the transfer of alleles between populations through migration.
What is genetic drift?
Genetic drift is random changes in allele frequencies in small populations.
What is the bottleneck effect?
The bottleneck effect is genetic drift after a drastic population reduction.
What is the founder effect?
The founder effect is genetic drift when a new population is established by few individuals.
What is Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium?
Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium describes allele frequencies in non-evolving populations.

The conditions are no mutation, random mating, no gene flow, large population, and no selection.
What is sexual selection?
Sexual selection is selection based on mating success rather than survival.
What is convergent evolution?
Convergent evolution produces similar traits in unrelated species due to similar environments.
What is divergent evolution?
Divergent evolution produces different traits in related species due to different environments.
What is coevolution?
Coevolution is reciprocal evolutionary changes in interacting species.
What is adaptive radiation?
Adaptive radiation is rapid diversification of a lineage into multiple species filling different niches.
What is molecular clock?
A molecular clock estimates evolutionary time based on mutation rates.
What is phylogeny?
Phylogeny is the evolutionary history and relationships among organisms.
What is a cladogram?
A cladogram is a diagram showing evolutionary relationships based on shared derived characteristics.
What is a common ancestor?
A common ancestor is an ancestral organism shared by two or more descendant species.
What is homology?
Homology is similarity due to shared ancestry.
What is analogy?
Analogy is similarity due to convergent evolution rather than common ancestry.
What is vestigial structure?
A vestigial structure is a reduced remnant of a feature that was functional in ancestors.
What is biogeography?
Biogeography studies the geographic distribution of species.
What is comparative anatomy?
Comparative anatomy studies similarities and differences in organism structures.
What is comparative embryology?
Comparative embryology studies similarities in embryonic development across species.
What is molecular evolution?
Molecular evolution studies changes in DNA and protein sequences over time.
What is the endosymbiotic theory?
The endosymbiotic theory proposes that mitochondria and chloroplasts originated from symbiotic bacteria.
What is the RNA world hypothesis?
The RNA world hypothesis proposes that RNA was the primary genetic material in early life.
What is abiogenesis?
Abiogenesis is the origin of life from non-living matter.
What are the three domains based on?
The three domains are based on ribosomal RNA sequences and cellular characteristics.
What distinguishes Archaea from Bacteria?
Archaea have different cell wall composition and unique membrane lipids compared to Bacteria.
What is horizontal gene transfer?
Horizontal gene transfer is the movement of genetic material between organisms other than parent to offspring.
What is punctuated equilibrium?
Punctuated equilibrium proposes that evolution occurs in rapid bursts followed by long stable periods.
What is gradualism in evolution?
Gradualism proposes that evolution occurs through slow continuous changes.
What is microevolution?
Microevolution is small-scale changes in allele frequencies within populations.
What is macroevolution?
Macroevolution is large-scale evolutionary changes resulting in new species or higher taxa.
What is artificial selection?
Artificial selection is selective breeding by humans to develop desired traits.
What is domestication?
Domestication is the process of adapting wild organisms for human use.
What is a biological species concept?
The biological species concept defines species as groups capable of interbreeding and producing fertile offspring.

Sexual reproduction combines genetic material from two parents producing genetically diverse offspring.
What is asexual reproduction?

What is parthenogenesis?
Parthenogenesis is asexual reproduction where eggs develop without fertilization.
What is binary fission?
Binary fission is asexual reproduction in prokaryotes where one cell divides into two.
What is budding?
Budding is asexual reproduction where a new organism grows from the parent.
What is fragmentation?
Fragmentation is asexual reproduction where a parent breaks into pieces that develop into new organisms.
What is regeneration?
Regeneration is the regrowth of lost body parts.
What is hermaphroditism?
Hermaphroditism is having both male and female reproductive organs.
What is fertilization?
Fertilization is the fusion of sperm and egg to form a zygote.
What is a zygote?
A zygote is the diploid cell formed by fertilization of gametes.
What is an embryo?
An embryo is an organism in early developmental stages after fertilization.
What is a fetus?
A fetus is a developing human from eight weeks after fertilization until birth.
What is gastrulation?
Gastrulation is embryonic development forming three germ layers.
What are the three germ layers?
The three germ layers are ectoderm, mesoderm, and endoderm.
What forms from ectoderm?
Ectoderm forms the nervous system and outer skin.
What forms from mesoderm?
Mesoderm forms muscles, bones, and circulatory system.
What forms from endoderm?
Endoderm forms the digestive tract and internal organs.
What is metamorphosis?
Metamorphosis is a dramatic change in body form during development.
What is complete metamorphosis?
Complete metamorphosis includes egg, larva, pupa, and adult stages.
What is incomplete metamorphosis?
Incomplete metamorphosis includes egg, nymph, and adult stages without a pupal stage.
What is senescence?
Senescence is biological aging and deterioration of function over time.

The Hayflick limit is the maximum number of times a cell can divide before senescence.
What is oxidative stress?
Oxidative stress is cellular damage caused by reactive oxygen species.
What is a free radical?
A free radical is a molecule with unpaired electrons that can damage cells.
What is an antioxidant?
An antioxidant is a molecule that neutralizes free radicals preventing cellular damage.
